Overview

The UCSD Mobile Cards app manages everything students need like Triton Cash, Dining Dollars, laundry, food, and more. It's supposed to be the Triton lifeline, but it had a 1-star rating in the App Store. I conducted 20 user interviews, ran statistically significant A/B testing, and redesigned the app from the ground up. The result: a SUS score improvement from 37 to 80.

The Problem

One of the app's core functions, adding funds to a Triton Cash account, requires jumping between 4 separate pages. Half of interviewed users had given up on the app entirely, using it only to check their dining balance and nothing else.

The Solution

I simplified the user flow, rebuilt the information architecture, and redesigned the interface to meet WCAG accessibility standards. The goal: make the app actually work for the students who depend on it every day.

Research

User Interviews

An example of a 1-star review in the App Store.


I conducted 20 in-depth interviews using open-ended questions, combining both dialogue and observation. Users spanned 4 grade levels to capture an accurate assessment across a broad demographic. I asked 4 initial questions, timed them completing 2 tasks on the app, and followed up with more questions. I learned users have two main goals: to check their Dining Dollars balance and add funds to their Triton Cash account for laundry. Students are frustrated with how many steps it takes to add money to their Triton Cash account; it took an average of 3 minutes. 10 out of 20 users stated they only use the app to check their dining balance and nothing else, indicating low functionality of its other features. Users were also given the System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire to complete. The SUS score of the current design is 37/100, which is considered poor usability. Users also wanted easier access to recent transactions. View the complete interview data here.

Problematic User Flow


A few screenshots of the deposit process.

The current user flow requires too many steps to complete simple tasks like viewing different account balances and depositing money. The Homepage only displays one balance, requiring users to jump around via the "back" button to see the others. Meanwhile, making a deposit requires users to move between 4 pages. Heuristic Evaluation

The current app fails many of Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and web accessibility guidelines. For example, there are different primary and drop-down buttons on the Home and Deposit pages. The appearance and behavior of the interface needs to be standardized. When an application adheres to standards, users know what to expect, learnability is increased, and confusion is reduced. Below are some of the issues I identified.

1. Visibility of System Status

Users should always be aware of what they are doing in an app, especially one that involves financial transactions.


2. Consistency and Standards

Applications should be internally consistent, like using the same buttons and colors, so users know what to expect.


3. Flexibility and Efficiency of Use

Provide users with shortcuts to speed up tasks.


4. Accessible Colors

Accessibility guidelines require text and its background to have a color contrast of at least 4.5:1 in order to be readable.


Prototype

Whiteboard Wireframes

I drafted the first wireframes on a whiteboard before using any design tool. The core decision was combining all account balances onto a single Home screen, eliminating the screen shuffling that frustrated users in the original app. A bottom navigation gives students access to Deposit, Accounts, and Transaction History from anywhere without losing their place.



Style Guide

I rebuilt the design system from scratch—replacing the flat blue palette with UCSD's official brand colors, establishing a type hierarchy using Teko and Source Sans, and standardizing the button system. Every color combination meets WCAG 4.5:1 contrast requirements.

Test

A/B Testing

A/B testing will empirically prove which design users prefer. I ran 20 A/B tests measuring user preference for making deposits. A is the current system (control) and B is my revamped design (variant). Users who saw the control had a conversion rate of 10%, and the other users who saw the variant had a 35% conversion rate. With a 95% statistical significance and p-value of 0.02, I can be confident that variant B is the preferred design. The SUS score for my design was 80/100.

Lessons Learned


Design decisions need to be evidence-based and validated through user testing—skipping that step is how a 1-star app gets built and stays that way. A 37 SUS score isn't an accident, it's what happens when no one asks the users. The most valuable lesson was learning to let the data speak. Whether or not an organization acts on your recommendations is outside your control.
